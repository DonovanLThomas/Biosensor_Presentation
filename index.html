Symposium Research Presentation

Remote Signals, Living Data: Heart & Brain from Anywhere

Designing a compact, biosensor-compatible foundation for less invasive organoid electrical recording.

Research with Asia Anderson

The Problem

Organoid signals are delicate. The recording setup should be, too.

Traditional electrical recording can depend on physical probes or needles entering the organoid environment to complete a circuit.

That approach can be invasive, limiting, and difficult to scale into compact or wireless systems.

We are trying to move beyond invasive probe-based setups and toward a wireless, biosensor-compatible way to record living electrical patterns.

In simpler presentation language: moving beyond the “caveman” way of sticking probes into the environment.

Research Goal

Build the electronics foundation for remote organoid electrical recording.

Prototype

ESP32-based system

Use compact, resourceful electronics that can send an electrical pulse and read a return signal.

Sensing

Biosensor compatible

Design around future integration with a biosensor interface rather than direct probe insertion.

Applications

Heart and brain organoids

Support long-term cardiac and neural organoid use cases without overclaiming current biological validation.

What Asia and I Worked On

This quarter was about building a strong technical starting point.

With Asia Anderson, I focused on the architecture and electronics needed before the full biological measurement loop can be tested.

  • System architecture brainstorming Mapping how organoid, biosensor, contact interface, ESP32, and dashboard could connect.
  • Hardware research Comparing electronics that are compact, low-cost, and compatible with future sensing goals.
  • ESP32 communication testing Writing and testing code that sends an electrical pulse and reads the return signal.
  • Probe-free interface exploration Looking toward anisotropic conductive tape as a contact path into the circuit.

Wireless Design Choice

BLE vs Wi-Fi is a design tradeoff, not a one-size-fits-all answer.

BLE

compact-friendly
  • Lower power draw
  • Phone and device friendly
  • Good fit for compact systems
  • May need more planning for data flow and dashboard tools

Wi-Fi

dashboard-friendly
  • Higher bandwidth
  • Direct path to laptop dashboard workflows
  • Useful for fast iteration during prototyping
  • More power use and network complexity

Next Steps

Turn the prototype foundation into a full measurement loop.

  1. Introduce anisotropic conductive tape into the circuit.
  2. Connect the biosensor into the full loop.
  3. Prove that an electrical signal can pass through the system.
  4. Measure how the organoid/biosensor environment alters the returned signal.
  5. Eventually validate with cardiac and neural organoid contexts.

Takeaway

We are laying the foundation for a more modern, wireless, and less invasive way to study living electrical signals.

This quarter’s work is design and prototyping, but that foundation is what makes future biological measurement possible.
